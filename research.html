Research

Focus, themes, and projects

My research focuses on ways in which institutional policy and our perspectives can lower barriers experienced by minoritized students in higher education systems. Across projects, I examine how students navigate identity, racism, and access to resources, and how institutions can design more culturally sustaining and information-accessible environments.

Core research themes

How my projects connect

Reducing institutional barriers through policy, practice, and information access
Higher education systems

I study how institutional rules, communication practices, and resource distribution shape students’ experiences of inclusion. I am especially interested in how institutions can lower barriers that disproportionately impact minoritized students.

Belonging, cultural meaning, and everyday campus experiences
Food, memory, care

I examine culturally familiar and comforting experiences as potential pathways to belonging, particularly for Korean American students navigating predominantly non-Korean institutional environments.

Identity, racism, and immigrant-generation experiences
Korean American students

My work explores how students describe and make sense of identity, the racialized contexts they move through, and how larger events (including COVID-era dynamics) shape their educational and social experiences.

AI for equity: lowering information barriers
Side project

As a complementary line of inquiry, I am interested in how AI could be used in education to reduce information barriers for minoritized students, especially in navigating complex institutional processes.

Research projects

Summary first, then details

Current project: Comfort food, familiar food, and belonging among Korean American students
Doctoral research

This project examines whether access to comfort food or familiar food is related to a sense of belonging among Korean American students. I focus on how culturally meaningful food experiences are shaped by institutional environments and how they may function as a form of care, recognition, or exclusion.

Ph.D. independent research: Interpretation and translation information on California K–12 district websites
Information provision and accessibility

This project analyzed how California K–12 school district websites communicate interpretation and translation services. I examined the availability and accessibility of language-access information, with attention to how institutional communication practices can either lower or reinforce barriers for immigrant-origin families.

Master’s thesis: Korean American students’ identity, immigrant generations, and racism during COVID
Identity development and racialized experiences

My master’s thesis examined how Korean American students identify themselves and how different immigrant generations experience and interpret racism. I was especially interested in the forms of racism students described during the COVID period and how these experiences shaped identity and belonging.

Side project: AI in education to reduce information barriers for minoritized students
Equity-oriented applications

This line of inquiry focuses on how AI tools might support students in understanding and navigating institutional information, particularly where policies and procedures are complex, fragmented, or difficult to access. I am interested in equity-centered design and implementation that prioritizes transparency, student agency, and responsible use.

Methods snapshot

How I approach inquiry

I primarily use qualitative and policy-relevant approaches, including interview-based inquiry, document and website analysis, and interpretive analysis focused on how students experience institutional environments. My work is oriented toward practical implications: how institutions can redesign policies, communications, and campus practices to reduce barriers and cultivate belonging.
